Question 4​
Read the passage given below and answer the questions that follow: [20] 
The rain had been falling incessantly for three days. The river, usually calm and placid, 
had turned into a raging torrent, its muddy waters licking hungrily at the riverbanks. In 
the small village of Shahpur, nestled in the foothills, the people watched with growing 
anxiety. The oldest resident, eighty-year-old Amma, had never seen the river this angry. 
"The river god is displeased," she muttered, her wrinkled face creased with worry. 
Young Arjun, however, was not one to wait and watch. He had seen the warning 
signs—the rising water level, the uprooted trees floating downstream. While the villagers 
debated and prayed, he took action. He gathered a group of his friends and started 
moving the livestock to higher ground. The goats bleated in protest, the cows mooed 
nervously, but the boys were persistent.

By evening, the water had entered the village. Homes made of mud and thatch began to 
dissolve like sugar in tea. Families scrambled onto rooftops, clutching their meager 
possessions. Arjun and his friends, instead of seeking safety themselves, used an old 
country boat to ferry people from their flooded homes to the relative safety of the hill 
slope. They worked through the night, guided only by lantern light and the desperate 
cries of their neighbors. 
When the rescue team finally arrived the next morning, they found a village 
half-submerged but its people safe. The officer in charge was amazed. "Who 
coordinated the evacuation?" he asked. The villagers, with gratitude in their eyes, 
pointed to a exhausted but smiling Arjun, slumped against a tree. The young boy, who 
had refused to be a passive victim, had become their unexpected hero. The river 
eventually receded, but the story of Arjun's courage would flow through the generations, 
a testament to the power of ordinary people in extraordinary times.
