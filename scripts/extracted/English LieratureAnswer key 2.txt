(i) Antony is speaking about Brutus. He calls him the noblest Roman because, unlike the 
other conspirators who acted out of envy, Brutus acted out of concern for the common 
good. [3] 
(ii) The other conspirators killed Caesar out of envy and personal ambition. Brutus alone 
joined them with honest thoughts, believing he was acting for the good of Rome. [3] 
(iii) Brutus has just died by suicide, running onto his own sword. He chose death over 
being paraded as a captive through Rome. [3] 
(iv) Antony says that Brutus was the only conspirator who acted for the general good. 
He proposes to give Brutus an honorable burial with full military honours, showing 
respect for his nobility even in death. [3] 
(v) Personal response required. Arguments for nobility: He was idealistic, motivated by 
principle, and showed consistency in his values. Arguments against flawed: His 
idealism made him impractical; he was easily manipulated and made poor strategic 
decisions. [4]

SECTION C (PROSE) 
Question 4 
(i) "He" is Martin, a thin, weak eleven-year-old boy. He is afraid of the elevator because it 
is old, creaky, and because he has encountered a strange, fat lady who rides it and 
makes him uncomfortable. [3] 
(ii) The woman is old, fat, and wears a dirty raincoat. She has small, pale eyes and 
always seems to appear when Martin is alone in the elevator. [3] 
(iii) The elevator stops between floors and the lights go out. Martin hears the woman 
breathing in the darkness, which terrifies him because he is trapped with his fear. [3] 
(iv) Martin's father dismisses his fears as foolish and tells him to take the stairs. This 
reveals their strained relationship—the father lacks empathy and understanding, and 
Martin feels isolated and unsupported. [3] 
(v) Personal response required. The ending is ambiguous, leaving readers to imagine 
what happens. This ambiguity is effective because it allows the horror to linger in the 
reader's imagination. [4] 
Question 5 
(i) The speaker is Adjoa, a young girl. Nana is her grandmother. [3] 
(ii) Nana thinks Adjoa's legs are too thin and slender. She believes such legs are not 
good for childbearing and that a woman's worth is measured by her ability to have 
children. [3] 
(iii) "Wanderlust" means a strong desire to travel and see new places. Nana calls it a 
disease because she believes a woman's place is at home, not wandering around. [3] 
(iv) Adjoa wins a running race at school. Nana is overjoyed and proudly declares, "saa, 
my granddaughter can do it, she can." She finally sees value in Adjoa's legs. [3] 
(v) The title celebrates female potential beyond traditional roles. The story conveys that 
girls should not be limited by societal expectations; they have abilities and talents that 
deserve recognition. [4]

(i) The speaker is Franz. M. Hamel is reading the French grammar lesson. It is special 
because it is the last French lesson they will ever have. [3] 
(ii) Franz finds it easy because he is finally paying attention, realizing the value of what 
he is about to lose. His regret and newfound appreciation make him focus. [3] 
(iii) M. Hamel is patient, gentle, and emotional. He does not scold Franz for being late 
and teaches with unusual calmness and seriousness. [3] 
(iv) M. Hamel writes "Vive La France!" ("Long Live France!") on the blackboard. This 
shows his love for his country and his language, and his despair at losing them. [3] 
(v) The child's perspective makes the story more poignant because it shows the loss of 
innocence and the realization of important truths through simple, honest observations. 
It makes the theme accessible and emotionally powerful. [4] 
 
SECTION D (POETRY) 
Question 7 
(i) The poet notices a tiny speck (a mite) on his white paper. He notices it because the 
paper is so white that any speck stands out clearly. [3] 
(ii) The speck suddenly moves and multiplies (seems to have legs or features). The poet 
muses that it is something like a tiny mite. [3] 
(iii) The poet spares the mite because he recognizes that it has a will to live and a mind 
of its own. It shows intelligence by trying to escape. [3] 
(iv) This means the mite demonstrated purposeful behavior, not just random movement. 
This is significant because it shows that even tiny creatures have consciousness and 
the desire to survive. [3] 
(v) The central theme is the value and dignity of all living things, no matter how small. 
The poem suggests that even the smallest creature deserves respect and has an 
inherent right to exist. [4]

(i) Bhishma Lochan Sharma is a singer. He is performing a raga (Bhairav) with great 
enthusiasm. [3] 
(ii) His wife faints at the very first note. This suggests his singing is so terrible or 
powerful that it overwhelms her completely. [3] 
(iii) The sky weeps (rains), the trees shake, and branches crash down. Personification 
makes the reaction humorous and exaggerated, emphasizing the disastrous effect of 
his singing. [3] 
(iv) The people flee, retire amazed, or faint. They cannot bear to listen and try to escape. 
[3] 
(v) The tone is humorous and satirical. The exaggerated reactions of nature and people 
create a comic effect, poking fun at bad singing without being cruel. [4]
