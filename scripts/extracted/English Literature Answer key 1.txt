SECTION B (DRAMA) 
Question 2 
(i) Caesar is speaking to the conspirators (Metellus Cimber, Brutus, Cassius, etc.) who 
are petitioning him to repeal the banishment of Publius Cimber. Caesar refuses because 
he believes his decisions should be firm and unchanging. [3] 
(ii) "Constant as the northern star" means that Caesar is fixed, unmovable, and 
unchanging in his decisions, just as the North Star appears fixed in the sky. This reveals 
Caesar's pride, arrogance, and belief in his own infallibility. [3] 
(iii) "There is no fellow in the firmament" means there is no star equal to the northern 
star in the sky. Caesar compares himself to this unique star, showing his belief that he is 
unmatched, supreme, and above all other men. [3] 
(iv) Immediately after this speech, the conspirators surround Caesar and stab him. 
Casca strikes first, followed by the others. Caesar's final words are: "Et tu, Brute? — Then 
fall, Caesar!" He is shocked and heartbroken that even Brutus has betrayed him. [3] 
(v) Personal response required. Arguments for strength: Caesar was standing by his 
principles and refusing to be swayed by emotional pleas. Arguments for weakness: His 
stubbornness and pride made him blind to danger and unwilling to listen to reason, 
which led to his downfall. [4] 
Question 3 
(i) Brutus is speaking to Cassius. They are in Brutus' tent at Sardis, having a heated 
argument about money and accusations of corruption. [3] 
(ii) Brutus reminds Cassius about the ides of March to emphasize that they killed 
Caesar for justice. He is accusing Cassius of now acting unjustly by taking bribes and 
corrupting their cause. [3] 
(iii) Brutus has accused Cassius of taking bribes and refusing to send him money to pay 
his soldiers. Cassius responds with anger and hurt, denying the accusation and 
reminding Brutus of their friendship and shared cause. [3] 
(iv) Messala brings news that Antony and Octavius have executed many senators in 
Rome and are marching with a powerful army towards Philippi. Brutus reacts with 
determination, seeing it as confirmation that they must fight. [3]

(v) Personal response required. Brutus: idealistic, moral, but impractical and rigid. 
Cassius: practical, shrewd, but sometimes self-serving. Arguments for either as the 
better leader can be accepted. [4] 
 
SECTION C (PROSE) 
Question 4 
(i) The narrator is at a photography studio. He has come to get his photograph taken. 
[3] 
(ii) The photographer rolls the machine into position, looks through the finder, and then 
physically adjusts the narrator's face by pulling at his ear. He does this to try to 
"improve" the narrator's appearance according to his own artistic standards. [3] 
(iii) The narrator feels uncomfortable and annoyed but remains passive. This reveals 
that he is patient, self-conscious, and perhaps lacks the confidence to protest. [3] 
(iv) When shown the final photograph, the narrator cannot recognize himself because 
the photographer has altered every feature. He is shocked, disappointed, and finally 
angry. [3] 
(v) Central theme: The conflict between artistic vision and personal identity; body 
shaming; the importance of accepting oneself. Personal response required. [4] 
Question 5 
(i) The person is Leonard Mead. He is walking alone through the city streets at night. 
[3] 
(ii) He compares himself to a hawk to emphasize his isolation and solitude. Like a hawk 
in empty country, he is the only moving thing in a deserted landscape. [3] 
(iii) It is November, at night. The setting creates a mood of loneliness, emptiness, and 
eerie silence, reflecting a dystopian society. [3] 
(iv) He is stopped by a police car (which is automated and has no human occupant) and 
arrested for walking without purpose. The car takes him to the Psychiatric Center for 
Research on Regressive Tendencies. [3] 

--- PAGE 4 ---
(v) Bradbury is criticizing the loss of individuality, the dominance of technology, and the 
suppression of human connection in modern society. The story warns against a future 
where people are isolated and controlled by technology. [4] 
Question 6 
(i) The speaker is Franz, a young schoolboy. It is his last French lesson because an order 
from Berlin has forbidden the teaching of French in the schools of Alsace and Lorraine; 
only German will be taught. [3] 
(ii) He regrets wasting time in the woods, missing lessons, and not taking his studies 
seriously. He wasted time chasing birds' nests and sliding on the river Saar. [3] 
(iii) His attitude changes from seeing books as tiresome burdens to cherishing them as 
old friends. This change occurs because he realizes he will never get to learn French 
again. [3] 
(iv) He notices that the classroom is unusually quiet and solemn. The village elders, 
including old Hauser, are seated at the back of the room. [3] 
(v) The title signifies the loss of language and cultural identity under occupation. The 
story conveys that language is an integral part of one's identity and that losing one's 
language means losing a part of oneself. [4] 
 
SECTION D (POETRY) 
Question 7 
(i) According to Longfellow, houses become haunted because people have lived and 
died in them. Unlike traditional frightening ghosts, his phantoms are "harmless" and 
glide peacefully. [3] 
(ii) The phantoms glide with "feet that make no sound." Their silent, gliding movement 
suggests they are peaceful, gentle, and non-threatening. [3] 
(iii) The stranger is someone who does not live in the house and lacks emotional 
connection to it. The poet can see the memories and spirits of those who lived there 
before because he is connected to the house's history. [3] 

--- PAGE 5 ---
(iv) Longfellow suggests that the living do not truly own property because the dead, 
through their memories and spirits, have a prior and continuing claim. The "memory of 
the dead" holds the title meaning the past always inhabits the present. [3] 
(v) Mood: peaceful, contemplative, nostalgic, comforting. Imagery: open doors, gliding 
phantoms, silent floors, the fireside. Personal response required. [4] 
Question 8 
(i) The king is watching a fight between lions. The nobles and ladies are seated around 
the arena, watching the spectacle. [3] 
(ii) The Count de Lorge sits among the nobles. He sighs for one of the ladies, indicating 
he is in love with her. [3] 
(iii) The lady throws her glove into the lion's den. She does this to test the Count's love 
and courage, expecting him to retrieve it. [3] 
(iv) The Count de Lorge descends into the arena, calmly retrieves the glove, and returns. 
However, instead of giving it back to the lady romantically, he throws it in her face. [3] 
(v) The king judges that the lady's action was cruel and thoughtless. The poem teaches 
that true love does not require reckless tests, and that cruelty disguised as a test of love 
is unacceptable. [4] 
Question 9 
(i) "Great tree" is a metaphor for a great soul or a loved one who has died. Its fall causes 
widespread disturbance—even distant rocks shudder, and powerful animals seek safety. 
[3] 
(ii) The lions and elephants symbolize even the strongest and most powerful beings. 
Their reaction shows that death affects everyone, regardless of their strength or status. 
[3] 
(iii) Humans experience shock, numbness, and a sense of being overwhelmed. They feel 
small, lost, and their senses are "eroded"—they cannot process what has happened. [3] 
(iv) This means that grief is so overwhelming that it goes beyond normal fear. People 
are so stunned by loss that even their ability to feel fear is diminished or altered. [3] 

--- PAGE 6 ---
(v) The central message is that while the loss of great souls is devastating, their 
memory and influence continue to shape and guide us. The poem offers hope by 
suggesting that we can become better because they existed. [4]
